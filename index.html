Case Study 02 · Content Creators

Rapid In-Person Design Sprint on Creator Publishing Flows

Focus Groups Co-Creation In-Person ~5 Business Days
⚡ The Impact

Findings from this study directly shaped the product and engineering roadmaps, aligning feature development with the highest-impact user needs. The research translated creator-driven UX insights into validated live prototypes, driving measurable gains in user satisfaction and adoption. Its rapid, highly collaborative design also elevated cross-functional alignment by bringing teams into direct contact with user feedback, significantly strengthening trust and investment in UXR.

The Research Challenge

A leading social media platform wanted to define a bold six-month product roadmap focused on launching "big bet" production tools for Content Creators. The goal was to identify high-impact opportunities that would meaningfully elevate the creator experience and differentiate the platform in a competitive market. To accelerate alignment and decision-making, we led an intensive one-week design sprint, bringing together cross-functional stakeholders across product, design, engineering, and strategy to rapidly explore, prototype, and validate breakthrough concepts.

My Role & Key Decisions

I moderated 8 in-person focus groups over 2 days with Content Creators with a live backroom audience of 30+ XFN observers. I leveraged subject matter expertise on Content Creators and the client's product ecosystem to gather actionable feedback on same-day feature concepts—meaning I received designs the morning of sessions, participated in EOD discussions with XFN observers to synthesize findings, and delivered same-day insights to drive real-time iteration. I also supported subsequent waves that advanced concepts from early ideation through iteration and usability testing.

Why In-Person Co-Creation?

The goal was to move fast and make real-time design decisions. Live group discussions and feedback enabled iterative prototyping, tighter team alignment, and deeper empathy for end users across product, design, and engineering.

Why Prioritize Speed?

In fast-moving organizations, the speed of insight delivery can matter more than perfecting the deliverable. A clear, actionable point of view delivered at the right moment often drives more impact than a highly polished report that arrives too late. In engineering- and product-led environments especially, I focus on delivering concise, decision-ready insights that match the pace of the build cycle and keep teams moving forward.

Reflections

The in-person format enabled deeper feedback and rapid iteration, with daily synthesis sessions unlocking faster decision-making than a traditional reporting cycle. Given the time-sensitive nature of feature development, we made a deliberate decision to prioritize speed and live collaboration over producing a comprehensive final report, trading polished documentation for immediate product impact. Subsequent waves transitioned to a virtual format to improve budget efficiency and layered in quantitative follow-ups to validate and scale the qualitative insights.

Case Study 01 · Content Creators

Creator Acquisition & Retention: From Pilot to National Launch

IDIs Focus Groups 8+ Wave Study Longitudinal GTM Research
⚡ The Impact

In early 2026, a leading social media platform launched a creator incentive program to wide public attention (covered by TechCrunch, CNBC, and Engadget). The campaign's name, value propositions, and program structure came directly from our research.

This was the culmination of 8+ research waves spanning about a year: from understanding first-time creator experiences, through iterative program refinement, to the GTM strategy that shaped the launch.

Internally, the research earned significant praise for delivering unusual clarity at a pivotal GTM moment: a decisive path forward that teams across the organization could rally behind. Externally, for the first time, the outcome was publicly visible: the 2026 program launched nationally, and the campaign language audiences encountered traced directly back to what we tested with creators in sessions.

The Research Challenge

A top social media platform piloted a creator incentive program in 2025 designed to attract established talent from competing platforms through structured bonuses for posting. The initial research goal was to understand creators' first-time experiences and identify what was driving retention versus attrition. As findings from each wave informed the next, the scope of the work expanded, ultimately shaping not just the program design, but the go-to-market strategy and public messaging for the platform's biggest creator initiative of 2026.

My Role & Key Decisions

I led every wave of this research, partnering closely with the client UXR across the full year of study.

In Waves 1 and 2, I conducted approximately 40 interviews at two timepoints: once during the program and again after, to capture how creator perceptions evolved over time. I synthesized findings into a single comprehensive report distributed across research, program, and product teams, which directly informed key program updates and shaped the scope of follow-up studies.

Across Waves 3 through 8, I shifted to a focus group format, conducting approximately 10 groups per wave (N=3 per group) with roughly 30 creators per wave. Throughout each wave, I partnered with the client UXR team to produce in-flight summaries during active fieldwork and a final end-of-wave report on completion, keeping the program team aligned and moving in real time rather than waiting on a single end-of-study deliverable.

Why Shift From IDIs to Focus Groups (N=3)?

IDIs were the right tool for Waves 1 and 2: mapping individual onboarding experiences, retention drivers, and emotional arcs required the depth and privacy of one-on-one conversations. But by Wave 3, the research questions had shifted. We were now tracking how program changes landed across cohorts, comparing reactions across creator segments, and understanding the social narratives forming around the platform, which are questions better answered in a group context.

Compared to traditional focus groups, triads give each participant substantial airtime while still generating the peer interaction that reflects how creators actually talk about platforms in the real world. Creators are inherently communal; their opinions about programs and platforms are shaped by what peers think and share, so the format more closely mirrors the context in which those opinions form.

Logistically, running 10 groups per wave with roughly 30 creators maintained the sample depth of the IDI phases while significantly compressing fieldwork time, which is what made rapid in-flight summaries and tight end-of-wave turnarounds possible across six consecutive waves.

Waves 1–2 — Pilot Evaluation:
Understood first-time creator experiences onboarding via the bonus program. Uncovered a critical misalignment between how the platform defined "effort" and how creators experienced it. Findings shaped onboarding flows, refined retention strategies, and established a framework for evaluating long-term incentive effectiveness that subsequent waves built on.

Waves 3–5 — Iterative Refinement:
Tracked program updates across subsequent cohorts across various program outcomes (~30 creators per wave). Assessed how changes landed, explored distinct audience segments, and built a cumulative understanding of retention and attrition drivers.

Waves 6–7 — Segment Exploration:
Deeper dives into specific creator segments and competitive platform comparisons, informing how the program needed to evolve to compete for high-value talent.

Wave 8 — GTM Strategy:
The final wave shifted from program evaluation to go-to-market readiness. We tested program names, value propositions, and messaging with established creators, directly informing the campaign language used at public launch.

GTM Wave Spotlight

The GTM strategy wave culminated in the campaign name, value propositions, and program structure that went to public launch (covered by TechCrunch, CNBC, and Engadget). This wave had three goals: identify the program name with the strongest resonance, validate the core value propositions, and understand what would make a creator say yes to joining versus walking away.

In focus groups, we tested five name concepts, walked creators through program details, had them rank and react to stimuli in real time, and compared the 2026 program directly to the prior year's program to understand perceived improvement. Creators also mapped their unprompted associations with the platform as a creator space, providing candid competitive context that shaped how the GTM messaging was eventually framed.

Reflections

The longitudinal nature of this work was its greatest strength. Each wave built on the last, but the synthesis across waves often happened informally. For a program of this scale and duration, I'd advocate for a dedicated cross-wave meta-synthesis: a structured effort to map how creator sentiment shifted over time and what drove those shifts. Though each individual report referenced past findings, a dedicated longitudinal artifact would have been as valuable to the strategy team as any individual wave report.

Case Study 03 · MR Owners & Prospects

Defining a New Concept for Mixed Reality Headsets

IDIs JTBD Analysis Mixed Methods 0→1 Concept Research
⚡ The Impact

Insights clarified the highest-value jobs the product must support and identified the attributes that make the product feel intuitive, achievable, and trustworthy. The work aligned stakeholders around the most viable use cases for early development, surfaced previously overlooked emotional criteria, and reframed internal conversations from technical feasibility to human desirability. Findings also guided next-phase concept exploration and highlighted critical friction points by segment, enabling more targeted follow-up research and feature prioritization.

The Research Challenge

A leading technology client sought to understand user expectations and define opportunity areas for a new 0→1 concept in Mixed Reality (MR). The two main goals were to identify the most valuable Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) and the key attributes needed to drive meaningful adoption and satisfaction. To explore these goals, we conducted a quant-qual study with MR owners and MR prospects.

My Role & Key Decisions

I led the qualitative phase end-to-end and partnered closely with the quantitative lead to merge both datasets into a cohesive insight framework. This included conducting 12 in-depth interviews, shaping the JTBD mapping and thematic clustering, and working directly with stakeholders to ensure outputs were actionable, relevant, and scalable. I translated observable behaviors and emotional patterns into program-level recommendations that helped the team re-evaluate which jobs and experiences the product should focus on first.

To synthesize across both methods, I grouped open-ended responses into core JTBD themes, mapped emotional versus functional goals by segment, assessed resonance across three early concept variants, and identified friction points by use case. This approach — rather than reporting on individual tasks in isolation — surfaced the shared motivations underneath them, and in doing so, inverted two of the team's core working hypotheses: the most common use cases turned out to be emotionally complex rather than functionally-driven, and users expected the product to serve multiple roles simultaneously, not one function at a time. The thematic mapping also revealed meaningful divergence between MR owners (who wanted seamless integration with existing tools and routines) and MR prospects (who sought novelty, inspiration, and low-friction delight) — a segment-level distinction that would have been easy to miss without synthesizing at the goal level rather than the task level.

Why This Methodological and Synthesis Approach?

We used a mixed-methods approach to balance breadth with depth. The survey helped surface a broad landscape of possible JTBD across a diverse sample, establishing a baseline for which functions and experiences to explore. IDIs allowed us to probe why those jobs mattered, how users envisioned the concept assisting in those jobs, and what emotional or behavioral context shaped their expectations.

From there, we grouped detailed user responses into larger thematic JTBD categories to reduce noise and identify shared underlying motivations. While users described a wide range of specific tasks, many mapped back to a smaller set of consistent functional and emotional goals. This layered synthesis made it easier to compare needs across segments, prioritize design implications, and avoid over-indexing on surface-level behaviors that were actually expressions of deeper, recurring jobs.

Case Study 04 · Content Creators & Social Media Users

Social Media Video Player Interface Refresh

IDIs Multi-Segment Concept Testing ~20 Business Days
⚡ The Impact

Insights defined the MVP for the unified viewing experience and creation tools, informed next steps for a unified monetization model, and uncovered mental models and comprehension gaps that improved onboarding alignment. The study provided strategic clarity for stakeholders while surfacing targeted areas for follow-up research.

The Research Challenge

A leading media platform sought to unify its video experience across short- and long-form content. To evaluate audience response, the team needed to understand the impact on diverse user segments across both content consumption and creation behaviors.

My Role & Key Decisions

I recruited and conducted 32 in-depth interviews with content creators and social media users to explore how they perceive and navigate current video formats, what they expect from content tools and how unification may impact expectations, and where confusion or friction may arise post-unification. I then delivered two interim topline reports and one final deep-dive report (including a live share-out) to the research and product teams.

Why Social Media Users AND Content Creators?

The two groups are not only distinct audience segments the platform must consider when evaluating product updates, but they also directly shape each other's outcomes. Poor video consumption experiences can limit creator success and negatively impact how creators perceive the platform. And vice versa, a negative creator experience can reduce content quality for viewers. Taking this dual perspective gave the team a more holistic view of risks and opportunities before making strategic product decisions.

Reflections

While findings were directionally strong, the broad scope was a deliberate tradeoff: the study needed to move quickly to stay ahead of engineering timelines, which meant covering multiple segments and topics within a single round of fieldwork rather than sequencing them. The cost was analytical depth. Covering both creators and users in the same guide required allocating session time across more topics per interview, which compressed how far we could go on any single theme. If I were scoping this from the start, I'd have designed a modular guide with a shared core and segment-specific deep-dives, or run separate but parallel rounds for each segment — preserving the dual-perspective insight while giving each audience more dedicated space. I'd also have built in a short quant pulse earlier in the process to pre-validate hypotheses and sharpen where the qualitative phase needed to go deepest.
